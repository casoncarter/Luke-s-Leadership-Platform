Module 1

PROBLEM SOLVING & OPPORTUNITY IDENTIFICATION

Anchoring Leaders: Elon Musk, Peter Drucker, Jack Dorsey

Tone: Blended Inspirational + Actionable (for a mature 17-year-old entering college)

Narrative: Seeing What Others Miss

Every entrepreneur begins with a moment — not of brilliance, but of noticing what others overlook and turning it into a path.

The world is full of problems disguised as “normal.” Most people accept them. Some complain about them. The exceptional learn to widen them into doorways.

This module teaches your son the first and most essential skill in entrepreneurship: the ability to identify real problems, define what truly matters, and transform a problem into a path.

And for this foundation, we turn to three leaders who mastered the art of seeing differently.

Elon Musk — Reimagine the Problem

Musk’s signature gift is not rockets or electric cars. It is the ability to look directly at a problem and ask:

“What is actually true here — and what have we simply accepted?”

He breaks challenges down to physics, cost curves, and natural constraints — and rebuilds from there. Everything else — tradition, industry norms, “the way things are done” — is noise.

For a young entrepreneur entering college, this is a powerful approach: don’t just solve the problem. Challenge the assumption and rebuild your understanding from the ground up.

Actionable takeaways for your son:

  • Don’t accept inherited definitions of a problem
  • Reduce challenges to first principles
  • Start with constraints, not conventions

Peter Drucker — Define the Real Need

Drucker, the father of modern management, believed that entrepreneurship begins with clarity.

Not “What product should I build?”

But “What need exists — and who is served?”

He taught leaders to observe systems, incentives, and human behavior with discipline and humility. Problems are rarely solved by intuition alone; they are solved by asking better questions.

Actionable takeaways for your son:

  • Learn to diagnose before you prescribe
  • Ask: “Who is the customer? What do they value?”
  • Distinguish symptoms from root causes

Jack Dorsey — Simplicity as an Opportunity Filter

Dorsey built products around one obsession: reducing friction.

Square existed because he saw small businesses blocked by complexity. Twitter succeeded because it stripped communication to something fast and simple.

His lesson: opportunities often live where things are unnecessarily hard.

Actionable takeaways for your son:

  • Find friction and remove it
  • Simplify the customer experience
  • Let constraints sharpen creativity

Exercises

  • Opportunity Journal: Write down 5 frustrations per day for one week. Choose one and analyze it with first principles (Musk), diagnosis (Drucker), and simplification (Dorsey).
  • “Why ladder” drill: For one chosen problem, ask “why?” five times to locate root cause.
  • Friction map: List steps a user must take to complete a task; highlight where effort spikes.

Reflection

Entrepreneurship begins with perception. What does your son notice that others normalize? If he trains this skill now, he will carry it into every class, internship, and career decision.

Module 2

CREATIVITY × ANALYTICAL THINKING

Anchoring Leaders: Reed Hastings, Tim Brown, Leonardo da Vinci
Tone: Thoughtful + Expansive + Structured (for a student who loves both logic and creativity)
Narrative: The Competitive Advantage of “Both/And”

Anchoring Leaders: Reed Hastings, Tim Brown, Leonardo da Vinci

Tone: Thoughtful + Expansive + Structured (for a student who loves both logic and creativity)

Narrative: The Competitive Advantage of “Both/And”

Most people think creativity and analytical thinking live on opposite sides of the brain. But modern entrepreneurship belongs to those who can do both: imagine something new and then build it with rigor.

This module teaches your son how to unite these two strengths — and how that fusion becomes a real competitive advantage.

Reed Hastings — Data + Taste

Netflix succeeded because Hastings blended data science with creative intuition. He used analytics to understand behavior, but he also invested in storytelling, culture, and product experience.

His lesson: the best builders measure what’s true, but they also sense what matters.

  • Use data to reduce uncertainty — not to avoid judgment
  • Hold opinions, but update them with evidence
  • Build feedback loops for both numbers and experience

Tim Brown — Design Thinking as a Method

Tim Brown (IDEO) teaches that creativity isn’t randomness — it’s a process. Design thinking is a repeatable method for generating ideas, testing them, and refining them through empathy and iteration.

  • Start with empathy: understand the user’s lived experience
  • Prototype quickly to learn cheaply
  • Iterate based on real feedback, not ego

Leonardo da Vinci — Curiosity Without Borders

Da Vinci had no categories. Art, science, anatomy, mechanics — all were one exploration. His lesson for your son: curiosity across domains is not a distraction. It’s an advantage.

  • Cross-train your mind by exploring outside your “major”
  • Keep a notebook for questions, sketches, and hypotheses
  • Make connections across disciplines

Exercises

  • “Both/And” Challenge: Pick one business idea. Write (1) the creative vision, then (2) the analytical model (assumptions, numbers, constraints).
  • Rapid prototypes: Create 3 versions of the same idea in 30 minutes (sketches, storyboards, UI screens, paper models).
  • Curiosity map: List 10 topics you’re curious about; circle 3 and design a one-week micro-study plan.

Reflection

The future belongs to builders who can think in systems and still see beauty, meaning, and human desire. If your son can integrate creativity with analytical rigor, he becomes rare.

Module 3

INNOVATION & DESIGN THINKING

Anchoring Leaders: Jeff Bezos, Steve Blank, Brian Chesky
Tone: Practical + Modern + Builder-focused
Narrative: Innovation Is a System

Anchoring Leaders: Jeff Bezos, Steve Blank, Brian Chesky

Tone: Practical + Modern + Builder-focused

Narrative: Innovation Is a System

Innovation is often romanticized as inspiration. In reality, innovation is a system: a disciplined way of discovering what people need and building solutions that scale.

This module teaches your son the core frameworks behind modern innovation and startup building.

Jeff Bezos — Customer Obsession

Bezos built Amazon around one principle: obsess over the customer, not the competition. He believed that if you make decisions from the customer backward, you will outlast cycles and competitors.

  • Start with the customer’s pain point
  • Build long-term trust through consistency
  • Use invention to reduce cost and friction

Steve Blank — “Get Out of the Building”

Blank teaches that you don’t “execute” a plan — you discover one. Entrepreneurs must test assumptions through real conversations and real behavior.

  • Assumptions are guesses until tested
  • Talk to users early, often, and honestly
  • Let evidence shape the product

Brian Chesky — Design + Trust

Airbnb succeeded because Chesky understood experience design and trust. He didn’t just build a marketplace; he built a feeling of safety and belonging.

  • Design the entire experience, not just the product
  • Trust is a feature
  • Details create loyalty

Exercises

  • Customer-backward memo: Write a “press release” from the future announcing your product and the customer problem it solves.
  • Assumption list: For an idea, list 10 assumptions; select the riskiest 3 and design simple tests.
  • Trust audit: Identify what a user must believe to adopt your product; list design elements that increase trust.

Reflection

Innovation isn’t magic. It’s curiosity, discipline, and iteration. The earlier your son learns this system, the faster he can build in the real world.

Module 4

BUSINESS MODELS & VALUE CREATION

Anchoring Leaders: Warren Buffett, Sara Blakely, Marc Benioff
Tone: Strategic + Practical + Customer-driven
Narrative: Value Isn’t Created by Ideas — It’s Created by Models

Anchoring Leaders: Warren Buffett, Sara Blakely, Marc Benioff

Tone: Strategic + Practical + Customer-driven

Narrative: Value Isn’t Created by Ideas — It’s Created by Models

Many young entrepreneurs fall in love with an idea. But ideas don’t create value on their own. Value is created by a business model: the structure that turns a solution into something sustainable.

This module teaches your son how to think about value creation, pricing, distribution, retention, and the engine that makes a company durable.

Exercises

  • Pick 3 companies you admire. Identify how each makes money, how it acquires customers, and what keeps customers loyal.
  • For an idea, write 3 business model versions (subscription, transaction, licensing) and compare tradeoffs.
  • Create a simple unit economics model (CAC, LTV, gross margin) using assumptions.

Reflection

A great business model turns a good idea into something that lasts.

Module 7

LEADERSHIP & TEAM BUILDING

Anchoring Leaders: Satya Nadella, Coach K, Sheryl Sandberg
Tone: High standards + Human leadership
Narrative: Culture Is the Invisible Operating System

Leadership is often misunderstood as charisma, authority, or confidence. In reality, leadership is behavior — repeated daily — that shapes how people think, act, and treat one another.

This module teaches your son that culture is not slogans or values printed on a wall. Culture is the invisible operating system that governs decisions when no one is watching.

Satya Nadella — From Know-It-All to Learn-It-All

Nadella transformed Microsoft not by strategy alone, but by reshaping how leaders behaved. He replaced internal competition with curiosity, empathy, and growth mindset.

  • Create psychological safety so people can learn and improve
  • Model humility at the top
  • Reward learning, not ego

Coach K — Standards Over Motivation

Coach K understood that motivation fades. Standards endure. His teams were built around clarity: roles, expectations, accountability, and trust.

  • Define what excellence looks like — explicitly
  • Hold everyone to the same standard
  • Care deeply, but never lower the bar

Sheryl Sandberg — Trust, Candor, and Belonging

Sandberg emphasized leadership through communication and trust. Strong teams are built when people feel heard, respected, and supported.

  • Give direct but empathetic feedback
  • Build allies, not silos
  • Create environments where people belong

Exercises

  • Write a one-page “team operating manual” outlining expectations, communication norms, and accountability.
  • Run a meeting with a written agenda, time blocks, and follow-ups.
  • Observe a team you’re part of: what behaviors are rewarded vs discouraged?

Reflection

Leadership is not a position — it’s the habits you model every day. Culture forms whether you design it or not.

Module 8

FINANCIAL LITERACY FOR ENTREPRENEURS

Anchoring Leaders: Ken Griffin, Warren Buffett, Indra Nooyi
Tone: Practical + Disciplined
Narrative: Numbers Tell the Truth

Entrepreneurship without financial literacy is intuition without verification. Numbers do not replace judgment — they sharpen it.

This module gives your son the financial fluency needed to make grounded decisions, avoid self-deception, and understand what truly drives sustainability.

Warren Buffett — Economic Reality Over Story

Buffett ignores hype and focuses on cash flow, margins, and durable advantage. His discipline is emotional as much as analytical.

  • Understand how value is actually created
  • Separate narrative from economics
  • Think long-term

Ken Griffin — Risk Lives in the Details

Griffin teaches that risk hides in assumptions. Understanding exposure requires precision, not optimism.

  • Know where downside lives
  • Stress-test assumptions
  • Respect uncertainty

Indra Nooyi — Capital Allocation as Strategy

Nooyi emphasized disciplined investment — allocating capital to what matters most.

  • Resources signal priorities
  • Invest deliberately, not evenly
  • Align numbers with values

Exercises

  • Build a simple P&L with assumptions.
  • Model unit economics (CAC vs LTV).
  • Identify your biggest financial blind spot.

Reflection

Financial literacy turns confidence into competence.

Module 10

ETHICS, RESPONSIBILITY & LONG-TERM THINKING

Anchoring Leaders: Paul Polman, Abraham Lincoln, Melinda French Gates
Tone: Serious + Values-forward
Narrative: The Future Is Shaped by Values

Entrepreneurship is power: the power to build products, shape behavior, influence culture, and move money and attention. Power always carries a moral dimension — whether the builder admits it or not.

This module teaches your son that ethics are not “a nice add-on.” They are a strategic and human necessity. In the long run, trust is the most valuable asset a leader can hold — and the easiest to destroy.

Paul Polman — Stakeholders, Not Just Shareholders

Polman argued that business should be built for durability — not quarterly optics. His leadership mindset was anchored in a simple belief: if you ignore long-term effects on people and the planet, the bill eventually arrives.

  • Define success beyond short-term metrics
  • Consider second- and third-order consequences
  • Build trust as an operating principle

Abraham Lincoln — Moral Clarity Under Pressure

Lincoln’s leadership is a masterclass in choosing principle over convenience. He navigated intense conflict while holding a steady moral center, understanding that difficult decisions become easier when values are non-negotiable.

  • Clarify what you will not compromise
  • Hold steady when incentives pull you off course
  • Lead with responsibility, not popularity

Melinda French Gates — Use Power to Expand Opportunity

Gates focuses on systems: health, education, inequality, and the structural barriers that determine who gets access to opportunity. Her leadership lesson is that ethics aren’t only about “avoiding harm” — they’re about intentionally doing good with the leverage you have.

  • Ask who benefits — and who is excluded
  • Design for dignity and access
  • Measure impact honestly, not sentimentally

Exercises

  • Values Constitution: Write a one-page document that defines 5–7 core values, what they mean in practice, and what violating them would look like.
  • Second-Order Map: Choose a product or business idea. List likely consequences across users, society, incentives, privacy, mental health, and fairness. Then design mitigations.
  • Case Study: Study one business ethics failure. Identify early warning signs and the “small compromises” that led to large consequences.
  • Pre-Commitments: Write 3 rules you will follow under pressure (e.g., truth in marketing, user consent, data minimization).

Reflection

Ambition without ethics becomes hunger. Ethics without courage becomes branding. The goal is character with competence — the kind of leadership people trust even when it is inconvenient.

Module 11

BRAND-BUILDING & STORYTELLING

Anchoring Leaders: Steve Jobs, Ralph Lauren, Oprah Winfrey
Tone: Meaning-driven + Craft-focused
Narrative: People Don’t Buy Products — They Buy Meaning

In a crowded world, quality matters — but clarity matters more. If people can’t articulate what you are and why you exist, they will forget you.

This module teaches your son that branding is not a logo. Branding is disciplined meaning: a consistent identity that makes people feel something specific — and trust that feeling over time.

Steve Jobs — Clarity Through Subtraction

Jobs understood that taste is a competitive advantage. He removed distractions until the product’s essence became obvious. His storytelling wasn’t decoration — it was focus.

  • Say one thing clearly rather than ten things vaguely
  • Design is communication
  • Consistency builds credibility

Ralph Lauren — Selling a World, Not a Shirt

Ralph Lauren built an empire by creating a coherent world: Americana, aspiration, elegance, familiarity. He didn’t market products; he offered identity. That’s the deepest form of brand loyalty.

  • Brands are stories people wear and inhabit
  • Details signal standards
  • Consistency over time creates trust

Oprah Winfrey — Trust Is the Brand

Oprah’s influence comes from credibility and emotional resonance. People believe her because her story feels authentic and her values feel stable. She models a lesson most entrepreneurs learn too late: trust is hard-earned and easily lost.

  • Credibility beats hype
  • Storytelling is emotional accuracy, not exaggeration
  • Brands grow when people feel seen

Exercises

  • Positioning Sentence: Write: “We help [who] achieve [outcome] by [how], unlike [alternative].” Make it sharp and specific.
  • Brand World: Create a moodboard of 10 images/words that define your brand’s world. Remove anything that doesn’t fit the story.
  • Before/After Story Arc: Write the customer’s life before your product, the moment they adopt it, and what becomes possible after.
  • Subtraction Drill: Remove 30% of the words from a homepage pitch. Make it simpler and stronger.

Reflection

Your product may solve a problem — but your brand tells people who you are. And who you are determines whether they trust you, remember you, and recommend you.

Module 12

DIGITAL TRANSFORMATION & AI FOR ENTREPRENEURS

Anchoring Leaders: Jensen Huang, Sam Altman, Fei-Fei Li
Tone: Modern + Practical + Responsible
Narrative: Leverage Is the New Differentiator

AI changes the shape of advantage. What used to require teams now requires systems. What used to take months can take days. But leverage is not automatically virtue — it amplifies whatever values and intentions are already present.

This module teaches your son how to think about AI like a builder: where it creates true leverage, how to implement it practically, and how to use it responsibly.

Jensen Huang — Infrastructure Wins

Huang’s leadership shows that technological revolutions are won by the people who build the infrastructure — the enabling platform that others depend on. The lesson for your son: don’t only chase apps; understand the stack beneath them.

  • Learn the fundamentals: data, compute, workflows
  • Build systems that scale
  • Think in platforms and ecosystems

Sam Altman — Distribution + Iteration + Speed

Altman’s playbook emphasizes rapid iteration and tight feedback loops. In modern markets, speed is not reckless — it’s a discipline: ship, learn, adjust, repeat.

  • Prototype quickly with AI assistance
  • Use feedback loops to avoid fantasy-building
  • Pair speed with guardrails

Fei-Fei Li — Human-Centered AI

Fei-Fei Li’s work emphasizes that technology should serve people — not reduce them. The lesson: responsible AI means designing for fairness, transparency, privacy, and dignity.

  • Bias and harm are often “invisible” until too late
  • Privacy is a design choice, not an afterthought
  • Human judgment remains essential

Exercises

  • Workflow Automation: Identify one repetitive workflow (research, scheduling, writing drafts, summarizing meetings). Design an AI-assisted process that saves time without losing quality.
  • Human-in-the-Loop System: Choose a decision (hiring, admissions-style evaluation, product recommendations). Design where AI assists and where humans must confirm.
  • Risk & Ethics Checklist: For any AI use-case: list data sources, privacy risks, bias risks, and misuse scenarios. Add mitigations.
  • Build a Mini-Prototype: Create a simple demo (mock UI, workflow diagram, or coded prototype) showing how AI improves the user experience.

Reflection

The best entrepreneurs won’t just use AI. They’ll use it with taste — balancing speed with responsibility, and leverage with integrity.
