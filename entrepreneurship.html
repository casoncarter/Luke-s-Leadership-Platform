Executive Summary

This platform is designed to equip a rising young entrepreneur with the mindset, tools, and ethical grounding needed to navigate college, career, and a rapidly changing world.

Across twelve modules, the curriculum blends

  • Analytical thinking
  • Creativity and design
  • Innovation frameworks
  • Strategic judgment under uncertainty
  • Financial literacy and disciplined execution
  • Leadership, storytelling, and culture-building
  • Ethics, responsibility, and long-term thinking
  • Digital transformation and AI fluency

Each module is anchored by three leaders whose lives and philosophies reinforce the core skill — building from problem-solving → execution → operating systems → thinking ethically → shaping the future.

What follows is a complete, integrated curriculum.

Module 1
PROBLEM SOLVING & OPPORTUNITY IDENTIFICATION

Anchoring Leaders: Elon Musk, Peter Drucker, Jack Dorsey

Tone: Blended Inspirational + Actionable (for a mature 17-year-old entering college)

Narrative: Seeing What Others Miss

Every entrepreneur begins with a moment — not of brilliance, but of noticing what others overlook and turning it into a path.

The world is full of problems disguised as “normal.” Most people accept them. Some complain about them. The exceptional learn to widen them into doorways.

This module teaches your son the first and most essential skill in entrepreneurship: the ability to identify real problems, define what truly matters, and transform a problem into a path.

And for this foundation, we turn to three leaders who mastered the art of seeing differently.

Elon Musk — Reimagine the Problem

Musk’s signature gift is not rockets or electric cars. It is the ability to look directly at a problem and ask:

“What is actually true here — and what have we simply accepted?”

He breaks challenges down to physics, cost curves, and natural constraints — and rebuilds from there. Everything else — tradition, industry norms, “the way things are done” — is noise.

For a young entrepreneur entering college, this is a powerful approach: don’t just solve the problem. Challenge the assumption and rebuild your understanding from the ground up.

Actionable takeaways for your son

  • Don’t accept inherited definitions of a problem
  • Reduce challenges to first principles
  • Start with constraints, not conventions

Peter Drucker — Define the Real Need

Drucker, the father of modern management, believed that entrepreneurship begins with clarity.

Not “What product should I build?”

But “What need exists — and who is served?”

He taught leaders to observe systems, incentives, and human behavior with discipline and humility. Problems are rarely solved by intuition alone; they are solved by asking better questions.

Actionable takeaways for your son

  • Learn to diagnose before you prescribe
  • Ask: “Who is the customer? What do they value?”
  • Distinguish symptoms from root causes

Jack Dorsey — Simplicity as an Opportunity Filter

Dorsey built products around one obsession: reducing friction.

Square existed because he saw small businesses blocked by complexity. Twitter succeeded because it stripped communication to something fast and simple.

His lesson: opportunities often live where things are unnecessarily hard.

Actionable takeaways for your son

  • Find friction and remove it
  • Simplify the customer experience
  • Let constraints sharpen creativity

Exercises

  • Opportunity Journal: Write down 5 frustrations per day for one week. Choose one and analyze it with first principles (Musk), diagnosis (Drucker), and simplification (Dorsey).
  • “Why ladder” drill: For one chosen problem, ask “why?” five times to locate root cause.
  • Friction map: List steps a user must take to complete a task; highlight where effort spikes.

Reflection

Entrepreneurship begins with perception. What does your son notice that others normalize? If he trains this skill now, he will carry it into every class, internship, and career decision.

Module 2
CREATIVITY × ANALYTICAL THINKING

+

Anchoring Leaders: Reed Hastings, Tim Brown, Leonardo da Vinci

Tone: Thoughtful + Expansive + Structured (for a student who loves both logic and creativity)

Narrative: The Competitive Advantage of “Both/And”

Most people think creativity and analytical thinking live on opposite sides of the brain. But modern entrepreneurship belongs to those who can do both: imagine something new and then build it with rigor.

This module teaches your son how to unite these two strengths — and how that fusion becomes a real competitive advantage.

Reed Hastings — Data + Taste

Netflix succeeded because Hastings blended data science with creative intuition. He used analytics to understand behavior, but he also invested in storytelling, culture, and product experience.

His lesson: the best builders measure what’s true, but they also sense what matters.

Actionable takeaways

  • Use data to reduce uncertainty — not to avoid judgment
  • Hold opinions, but update them with evidence
  • Build feedback loops for both numbers and experience

Tim Brown — Design Thinking as a Method

Tim Brown (IDEO) teaches that creativity isn’t randomness — it’s a process. Design thinking is a repeatable method for generating ideas, testing them, and refining them through empathy and iteration.

Actionable takeaways

  • Start with empathy: understand the user’s lived experience
  • Prototype quickly to learn cheaply
  • Iterate based on real feedback, not ego

Leonardo da Vinci — Curiosity Without Borders

Da Vinci had no categories. Art, science, anatomy, mechanics — all were one exploration. His lesson for your son: curiosity across domains is not a distraction. It’s an advantage.

Actionable takeaways

  • Cross-train your mind by exploring outside your “major”
  • Keep a notebook for questions, sketches, and hypotheses
  • Make connections across disciplines

Exercises

  • “Both/And” Challenge: Pick one business idea. Write (1) the creative vision, then (2) the analytical model (assumptions, numbers, constraints).
  • Rapid prototypes: Create 3 versions of the same idea in 30 minutes (sketches, storyboards, UI screens, paper models).
  • Curiosity map: List 10 topics you’re curious about; circle 3 and design a one-week micro-study plan.

Reflection

The future belongs to builders who can think in systems and still see beauty, meaning, and human desire. If your son can integrate creativity with analytical rigor, he becomes rare.

    Module 5
    EXECUTION, OPERATING SYSTEMS & DISCIPLINE
    Click to open the full module curriculum, frameworks, and exercises.
    +

    Anchoring Leaders: Ray Dalio, James Clear, Jeff Weiner

    Tone: Systems-driven + Practical

    Narrative: Talent Doesn’t Scale — Systems Do

    Big goals don’t fail because people lack ambition. They fail because they lack systems.

    This module helps your son develop a personal operating system — habits, feedback loops, and discipline that compound.

    Ray Dalio — Principles + Feedback

    Dalio built Bridgewater by turning his thinking into principles and systems. His idea: pain + reflection = progress.

    Takeaways

    • Write principles that guide decisions
    • Create feedback loops (people, metrics, reflection)
    • Turn mistakes into structured learning

    James Clear — Habits as Identity

    Clear teaches that habits are not just actions — they are votes for the type of person you want to become.

    Takeaways

    • Design your environment to support behavior
    • Make habits small and consistent
    • Focus on identity (“I am the kind of person who…”)

    Jeff Weiner — Compassionate Management

    Weiner built LinkedIn with a focus on compassionate leadership and disciplined execution — high standards without losing humanity.

    Takeaways

    • Lead with clarity, but treat people with dignity
    • Time-block priorities
    • Build routines that protect deep work

    Exercises

    • Create your “Operating System” document: principles, values, routines, and decision rules.
    • Weekly review: wins, losses, lessons, next actions.
    • Environment audit: remove friction for good habits; add friction for bad ones.

    Reflection

    Consistency is a superpower. If your son builds a system now, he will carry it through college and beyond.

    Module 6
    FINANCE, CAPITAL & INVESTOR THINKING
    Click to open the full module curriculum, frameworks, and exercises.
    +

    Anchoring Leaders: Ken Griffin, Howard Marks, Cathie Wood

    Tone: Clear + Sophisticated (but teachable)

    Narrative: Money Is Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

    Finance isn’t just math. It’s judgment. It’s the discipline of making decisions under uncertainty and managing risk with humility.

    This module teaches your son to think like an investor: probability, downside protection, and long-term compounding.

    Ken Griffin — Edge and Discipline

    Griffin built Citadel on rigorous research, risk management, and relentless learning. His lesson: intelligence without discipline is noise.

    Takeaways

    • Risk management is as important as upside
    • Build research habits
    • Decision quality matters more than outcomes

    Howard Marks — Second-Level Thinking

    Marks teaches that markets reward those who think beyond the obvious. “Second-level thinking” means asking: what is priced in? what could surprise?

    Takeaways

    • Separate fundamentals from sentiment
    • Look for mispricing and psychology
    • Understand cycles

    Cathie Wood — Conviction + Time Horizon

    Wood invests in disruptive innovation with deep conviction and long time horizons. Her lesson: the future is shaped by technology, but conviction must be backed by research.

    Takeaways

    • Time horizon is a strategy
    • Learn to underwrite a thesis
    • Conviction must be earned

    Exercises

    • Build an “investment memo” for one company: thesis, risks, valuation logic, time horizon.
    • Probability thinking: assign probabilities to 3 outcomes and compute expected value.
    • Risk map: identify worst-case scenarios and how to mitigate them.

    Reflection

    Finance teaches humility. The goal is not certainty — it’s disciplined thinking in uncertainty.

    • Empathy increases performance

    Takeaways

    • Build trust through consistency
    • Hold people accountable with respect

    Sandberg emphasizes empowerment: hiring well, setting clear expectations, and giving people ownership.

    Takeaways

    • Great leaders create other leaders
    • Clarity enables autonomy
    • Execution requires alignment

    Exercises

    • Culture audit: what behaviors are rewarded in your current teams (school, sports, clubs)?
    • Leadership practice: run a meeting with a clear agenda and follow-up actions.

    Module 9
    NEGOTIATION, INFLUENCE & POWER DYNAMICS
    Click to open the full module curriculum, frameworks, and exercises.
    +

    Anchoring Leaders: Chris Voss, Barack Obama, Sara Blakely

    Tone: Tactical + Ethical + Confident

    Narrative: Outcomes Are Negotiated

    In business — and life — outcomes are negotiated. Negotiation isn’t manipulation; it’s understanding incentives, psychology, and leverage.

    Key skills

    • Active listening
    • Tactical empathy
    • BATNA (Best Alternative)
    • Anchoring and framing

    Exercises

    • Practice “mirroring” and “labeling” in everyday conversations.
    • Before a request, write your BATNA and the other person’s BATNA.
    • Role-play a negotiation: internship offer, group project roles, or club leadership position.

    Reflection

    Confidence grows when preparation becomes a habit. Negotiation rewards calm, empathy, and clarity.
